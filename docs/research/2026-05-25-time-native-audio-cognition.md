# Time-Native Audio Cognition — Research-Direction Memo

**Date:** 2026-05-25
**Thesis under test:** Time-domain audio cognition needs architectures genuinely native to time — multi-scale, predictive, "thick-now" — rather than spectrogram-based vision-derived stacks. *Convert-time-to-space is a category error.*

**Epistemic conventions in this memo:** Every nontrivial empirical claim is tied to a named source. arXiv IDs / DOIs are given where verified this session. `[UNVERIFIED]` marks a paper I could not confirm exists at the stated identifier (the work may exist; the *identifier* is what's unverified). Contests are named with both sides. I mark genuine uncertainty rather than smoothing it.

**One honest framing up front.** The thesis is a *good organizing bet*, not a settled result. The strongest evidence *for* it is indirect (failures of spectrogram stacks at long range, the perceptual role of phase in transients, predictive-coding's grip on music). The strongest evidence *against* a strong version of it is that the dominant audio successes of the last five years — wav2vec2, AudioMAE, A-JEPA, MERT — are *not* time-native by this definition and still win benchmarks. The category-error claim is therefore best read as "the current benchmark set under-weights the regime where time-nativeness pays," which §5 and §9 argue is literally true (most benchmarks cap at 10 s). The interesting research is at the seam.

## §1. Phase-aware representation

**The single most important distinction — and it is almost never drawn explicitly — is between models that *preserve* phase for reconstruction and models that *use* phase as an information-bearing feature for the downstream task.** Almost everything in the "phase-aware" literature is the former. Genuine use of phase as cognition is confined to two niches.

**What's actually deployed.** Time-domain neural codecs (SoundStream, EnCodec, DAC) never represent phase explicitly — they map waveform→latent→waveform, so phase is *implicit* in the reconstructed samples and the network is never asked to reason about it. Magnitude-spectrogram pipelines (the classic mel→vocoder stack) *discard* phase and re-estimate it at synthesis time (Griffin–Lim, or a neural vocoder). The recent move is back toward explicit complex/STFT modeling: **Vocos** (arXiv 2306.00814) generates STFT magnitude *and* phase and synthesizes via inverse STFT, matching time-domain vocoder quality at >10× speed; **SpectroStream** (arXiv 2508.05207, Google DeepMind) is a 48 kHz stereo codec operating in the time-frequency domain whose multichannel "delayed-fusion" exists specifically to preserve *cross-channel phase consistency*. Note what phase is for in all of these: **reconstruction fidelity, not task information.** Vocos predicting phase makes audio sound right; it does not make a classifier hear better.

**The perceptual question is genuinely contested, and the contest is about window length — not a yes/no.** The founding result is Wang & Lim 1982, *"The unimportance of phase in speech enhancement"* (IEEE TASSP 30(4):679–681): at the 20–40 ms windows standard in speech processing, more accurate phase estimation is unwarranted. This calcified into "phase doesn't matter." Paliwal et al. 2011, *"The importance of phase in speech enhancement"* (Speech Communication; ScienceDirect S0167639310002086) directly rebutted it: with appropriate analysis windows and processing, phase carries substantial intelligibility information. The cleanest synthesis is the 2022 JASA-EL / arXiv 2203.16222 result *"Phase-aware deep speech enhancement: it's all about the frame length"*: **the value of phase grows monotonically as frames shorten, and transient structure lives in the phase spectrum while magnitude captures slowly-varying content.** This is the strongest single fact in favor of the memo's thesis: *the spectrogram's standard windowing literally throws away the part of the signal that encodes fine temporal events.* That is the category error, made concrete.

**Where phase is genuinely *used* (the real signal in the noise).** Two domains demonstrate phase as discriminative information, not reconstruction nuisance: (1) **anti-spoofing / synthetic-voice detection** — complex-valued CNNs with explicit ablations showing the network learns to *use* phase to catch vocoder artifacts (arXiv 2308.11800; broader treatment in arXiv 2510.09926, *"Phase-Aware Deep Learning with Complex-Valued CNNs"*); synthesis pipelines leave phase fingerprints that magnitude hides. (2) **Sound-event localization** — interchannel phase *is* the spatial cue; discarding it discards direction. A useful survey of the modeling landscape is arXiv 2505.08694, *"A Survey of Deep Learning for Complex Speech Spectrograms."* Complex-spectrum *codecs* are now emerging too (ComplexDec, complex-spectrum neural codec — [UNVERIFIED] arXiv ID; found via secondary reference only).

**Bottom line for §1.** Phase is being modeled more than ever, but overwhelmingly as a *reconstruction target*. The open, under-exploited claim — that monaural phase carries downstream-relevant *temporal-event* information (onsets, attack transients, microtiming) that magnitude-trained models are structurally blind to — is supported by the frame-length result and the anti-spoofing ablations but has **not** been tested as a general cognitive proposition. (See Experiment 1.)

## §2. Multi-scale temporal architecture

**Claim I'll defend: "multi-scale" in current audio SSMs is *imposed by hand*, not *learned*. The continuum of timescales is assumed, not achieved.**

**The two mechanisms in use are both bolt-ons.** (1) **Downsampling hierarchies.** SaShiMi (*"It's Raw!"*, arXiv 2202.09729) — the canonical time-native audio architecture — gets its multi-scale property from *tiered downsampling*: a top tier at the raw rate, lower tiers on pooled/downsampled signal, each tier a stack of S4 blocks. The multi-scale-ness is in the pooling schedule, a hyperparameter, not in the dynamics. (2) **Dual/multi-window fusion.** In music, SongFormer (arXiv 2510.02797) fuses a 30 s and a 420 s self-supervised representation to "jointly capture fine-grained and long-range dependencies." Again: two fixed windows, concatenated. Neither learns *which* timescales matter.

**The time-constant problem, precisely.** Vanilla Mamba (arXiv 2312.00752) has a learned discretization step Δ that sets the effective timescale of state decay — but Δ is **state-shared**: one timescale parameter governs the whole hidden state. As the follow-up literature puts it, a shared Δ "makes features uniformly ignored or considered for all states," limiting the model's ability to carry *diverse* temporal cues simultaneously. Audio is the worst case for this: a single 5-second piano phrase contains 1–5 ms attack transients, ~100 ms phone/note events, ~500 ms beats, and ~10 s phrase structure — four decades of timescale that a single shared decay cannot represent without compromise. Hyena (arXiv 2302.10866) sidesteps decay entirely with implicit long convolutions and *unrestricted* context, but "unrestricted" is not the same as "multi-scale": a long filter is one filter, not a bank of time constants. **Liquid-S4** and per-state-Δ variants (e.g., SeRpEnt, *"Selective Resampling for Expressive State Space Models,"* arXiv 2501.11729 — abstract not deep-read) are the live attempts to give *different* states *different* time constants, which is the right direction, but I found **no audio-domain demonstration** that a learned multi-timescale state bank beats either a downsampling hierarchy or a tuned single-scale model on a task that *requires* simultaneous fine + coarse resolution.

**Where the real gap is.** Not "can SSMs do long context" (they can; that's solved). The gap is **simultaneity**: a single forward pass that resolves microtiming (ms) and form (minutes) *at once*, with the timescales *learned from data* rather than set by a pooling schedule. No paper I found claims this for audio. That absence — not a positive result — is the §2 finding. (See Experiment 2.)

## §3. Predictive-coding training objectives in audio

**The honest status: predictive objectives are *real and old* in audio (older than in vision), demonstrably learn useful structure, but there is essentially no evidence that the *predictive* framing specifically improves *long-range temporal* cognition over masked-reconstruction. The benefit that's demonstrated is representation quality for classification.**

**Audio-specific, empirically demonstrated (not JEPA generalities):**
- **CPC** (van den Oord et al. 2018, arXiv 1807.03748) was *introduced on audio*: predict future latents via a probabilistic contrastive (InfoNCE) loss; phonetic structure emerged on LibriSpeech without labels. This is the empirical bedrock — predicting the near future in latent space yields linguistically structured representations.
- **APC** (Chung & Glass, arXiv 1910.12607; Interspeech 2019) is the generative cousin: regress the actual future frame. Demonstrated that lower layers carry speaker info, upper layers carry phonetic content — a learned hierarchy from a pure next-frame objective.
- **Hierarchical CPC (HCPC)** (*"Capturing Temporal Dependencies Through Future Prediction for CNN-Based Audio Classifiers,"* ICASSP 2021 — title/venue verified, [UNVERIFIED] arXiv ID) is the one paper I found that explicitly targets *multi-level* temporal dependencies with a predictive loss and shows consistent gains on music + environmental audio. It's the closest existing thing to the memo's program, and it's small/underexplored.

**The JEPA wave, assessed skeptically:**
- **A-JEPA** (arXiv 2311.15830) and **Audio-JEPA** (arXiv 2507.02915) bring latent-space masked prediction to audio. I deep-read A-JEPA's abstract: it predicts *latent representations* of masked spectrogram regions with a curriculum, time-frequency-aware mask. Two things matter for this memo. (1) **It operates on spectrograms with a ViT** — i.e., the flagship "predictive" audio model is built squarely on the vision-derived spectrogram stack the thesis calls a category error. (2) Its headline claim is **state-of-the-art classification**; I found **no temporal-specific result** (e.g., long-range structure, timing discrimination) demonstrating that the predictive objective bought *temporal* cognition rather than generic representation quality. That gap is the §3 finding, stated as a falsifiable negative: *as of this writing, no audio paper shows a predictive (latent-future) objective beating masked reconstruction on a task whose difficulty is specifically long-range temporal.*

**Brain-side corroboration (separate evidence stream).** Caucheteux & King and follow-ups argue the *brain* does predictive coding of speech, and "Language experience shapes predictive coding of rhythmic sound sequences" (eLife 91636) shows predictive coding of rhythm is experience-dependent. A general "Learning Long-Range Dependencies with Temporal Predictive Coding" (arXiv 2602.18131) exists outside audio. These motivate the objective biologically; they do not yet validate it as an *engineering* win for temporal audio.

## §4. Brain-aligned audio models — and the controversy, told straight

**The headline correction this memo can make: the "untrained-network-predicts-the-brain" critique that the brief attributes to the audio literature is, in its strong form, a *language*-domain finding. The audio evidence actually cuts the other way.** This is the most important thing in §4 and it is routinely conflated.

**The positive results.**
- **Kell et al. 2018** (Neuron 98(3):630–644.e16; doi 10.1016/j.neuron.2018.03.044; PMID 29681533): a task-optimized CNN trained on speech + music recognition matched human behavior, made human-like errors, predicted auditory-cortex fMRI *better than spectrotemporal-filter models*, and showed a hierarchy (intermediate layers→primary cortex, late layers→non-primary). Foundational.
- **Millet et al. 2022** (NeurIPS; arXiv 2206.01685): wav2vec2 vs fMRI of 412 subjects; brain-like representations emerge from **~600 h of speech — comparable to infant exposure** — with a functional hierarchy matching cortex. The data-efficiency point is biologically important.

**The controversy, both sides, precisely:**
- *Language domain (the actual origin of the untrained critique):* **Schrimpf et al. 2021 PNAS** (118(45):e2105646118) found untrained models with a trained linear readout predict the language network well above chance, and crucially **untrained scores predict trained scores at r = 0.74**. Follow-ups push further — *"Brain-Like Language Processing via a Shallow Untrained Multihead Attention Network"* (arXiv 2406.15109) and the pointed critique *"What Are LLMs Mapping to in the Brain? A Case Against Over-Reliance on Brain Scores"* (arXiv 2406.01538). The worry: brain-score may reflect architecture + tokenization + dataset statistics more than learned computation.
- *Audio domain (where the brief's framing breaks):* I deep-read **Tuckute, Feather, Boebinger & McDermott 2023, PLOS Biology** (21(12):e3002366; doi 10.1371/journal.pbio.3002366; biorxiv 2022.09.06.506680). Two findings are decisive. (1) The "untrained baseline" in *audition* is **not** a random network — it's a hand-built **SpectroTemporal modulation model** of the cochlea. (2) Their actual random-control — **permuted-weight networks** — produced worse predictions than trained models and **"in no case did they outpredict the baseline."** In other words: *in audio, task training matters; random nets do not predict cortex well.* This is the **opposite** of the language result.

**So the honest §4 statement:** the untrained-baseline skepticism is real and well-evidenced *for language*, where architecture alone gets you surprisingly far. **It does not transfer to audition**, where the McDermott-lab evidence says optimization is load-bearing. Anyone (including the framing of this very task) who cites "Schrimpf untrained critique" against audio brain-alignment is importing a language result into a domain that contradicts it.

**Don't oversell the audio side either.** Tuckute et al.'s own caveats: only **about half** of engineering models beat the SpectroTemporal baseline (some underperform it badly, and they couldn't cleanly attribute why); representational-similarity correlations sit **well below the noise ceiling and "not much higher than the baseline model"**; and models trained on **speech-in-noise** beat clean-speech models (p<0.001), implying current training objectives miss constraints real audition imposes. Translation: trained DNNs are the best models we have *and* they leave large, unexplained variance. Both are true.

## §5. Music as a temporal benchmark

**Why music is the right stress test (the strong version of the claim).** Music is the only widely-available signal that is *simultaneously* structured at four decades of timescale (microtiming ms → beat ~100s ms → phrase ~seconds → form ~minutes) **and** comes with strong, measurable human predictive responses. Two pillars:

1. **Prediction is the substance of music cognition, and it's been formalized.** Pearce's **IDyOM** (Information Dynamics of Music; Pearce 2012, *Topics in Cognitive Science* 4(4):625–652, doi 10.1111/j.1756-8765.2012.01214.x; Pearce 2018, *Annals NYAS*, doi 10.1111/nyas.13654) is an unsupervised variable-order Markov model that learns melodic structure by exposure and predicts human note-by-note expectation (surprise, uncertainty) — cited in hundreds of behavioral/neural studies. It is, in effect, a *predictive-coding model of music that already works on humans* — and it is bottom-up and time-native by construction. Any time-native audio model has a ready-made human-aligned target here.

2. **Groove gives a quantitative, non-trivial prediction signal.** Witek et al. 2014 (PLOS ONE 9(4):e94446; doi 10.1371/journal.pone.0094446): the desire to move and pleasure follow an **inverted-U in syncopation** (medium syncopation maximal) — Berlyne optimal-complexity, operationalized. Microtiming deviations of ~5–50 ms systematically shape groove (swing/jazz analysis, *Sci. Rep.* 2019, doi 10.1038/s41598-019-55981-3). This is a temporal phenomenon with no spectral correlate — magnitude spectrograms at standard windows cannot even represent the relevant deviations.

**Benchmarks that exist.** **MARBLE** (arXiv 2306.10548, NeurIPS 2023 D&B): 14 tasks / 8 datasets, the standard MIR representation benchmark. **Beat This!** (arXiv 2407.21658, ISMIR 2024): SOTA beat/downbeat tracking that finally generalizes across genres *without* a Dynamic Bayesian Network post-processor — but its own honest caveat is telling: it still fails on under-represented genres and **does worse on *continuity* metrics**, i.e., on sustained temporal coherence. **Groove MIDI Dataset** (Magenta; 13.6 h expressive drumming; E-GMD expanded; GrooVAE / arXiv 1905.06118) provides aligned microtiming ground truth — the rare dataset where the time-native target is *labeled*.

**Benchmarks that are missing (the real §5 finding).** Temporal cognition is essentially *unmeasured at scale* because the benchmarks are short. The BASS AI benchmark paper (arXiv 2602.04085) states it plainly: prior benchmarks like MusicCaps cap at **10-second** excerpts and SDD at **two minutes**, *"preventing evaluation of long-range musical structure"*; long-form generation (YuE, arXiv 2503.08638) is noted to go "repetitive or directionless" past a minute. **We do not have a standard benchmark for (a) minutes-scale structural prediction, or (b) microtiming/groove prediction evaluated against human ratings.** The measurement gap *is* the research gap: you cannot show time-nativeness pays if the benchmark truncates the regime where it would.

## §6. Biological frontends — engineering-useful, or biology-fetishism?

**Verdict: mostly fetishism on mainstream tasks, with two genuine engineering niches. I'll defend that honestly in both directions.**

**The deflationary evidence is strong and should not be soft-pedaled.** LEAF (arXiv 2101.08596, ICLR 2021) claimed a learnable frontend beats mel-filterbanks across eight tasks. The direct reproduction — pointedly titled **EfficientLEAF: "A Faster LEarnable Audio Frontend of Questionable Use"** (arXiv 2207.05508, EUSIPCO 2022) — found that with sane reimplementation, **both LEAF and EfficientLEAF fail to consistently outperform a fixed mel filterbank**, and the original advantage narrows to noise. This is the cautionary pattern for the whole family: learnable frontends tend to *converge toward mel* and the headline gains often don't survive independent reimplementation. SincNet (arXiv 1808.00158, Ravanelli & Bengio) is elegant — parameterize first-layer filters as band-pass sincs, learn only cutoffs — and helps for speaker ID where narrow-band pitch/formant cues matter, but it is not a general win over mel either.

**The genuine niches (where bio frontends earn their keep):**
1. **Differentiable, parameter-fittable cochlea models** — the real recent revival. **CARFAC v2** (Lyon, arXiv 2404.17490) now ships in MATLAB/NumPy/**JAX**, is fully differentiable, runs at real-time-factor 0.02–0.11, and has been used to **fit individual hearing-loss profiles via gradient descent** (per-frequency outer-hair-cell loss). That is a concrete engineering capability mel cannot provide: a personalizable, mechanistically interpretable front end for audiology and hearing-aid design.
2. **Regimes where the bio model's *nonlinearities* are the point** — cochlear compression/adaptation for speech-emotion and pitch-dominated tasks (cochleogram + CARFAC for SER, *Biomimetics* 2025), and spiking front-ends (Spiking-LEAF — [UNVERIFIED] this session) for neuromorphic/low-power deployment, where the value is energy and latency, not accuracy.

**Honest synthesis.** If the question is "does a cochlea-faithful frontend beat mel on AudioSet/ESC-50?", the evidence says *no, not reliably* — that pursuit is largely fetishism. If the question is "is a differentiable cochlea now a *useful tool*?", the answer is *yes, for personalization, interpretability, transient/temporal fidelity, and low-power* — distinct from raw benchmark accuracy. The memo's thesis is best served not by "use a cochlea because it's biological" but by the specific, testable sub-claim that cochlear temporal *fine structure* (preserved by these models, discarded by mel) matters for timing tasks — which is §1's phase point in the frontend.

## §7. Active listening

**I ran targeted searches to confirm absence. Result: the *embodied/spatial* form exists and is healthy; the *representation-learning* form — a model that allocates its own sampling of the time-frequency plane to reduce predictive uncertainty — is essentially absent. That gap is the finding.**

**What exists (embodied active audition):** the live literature is about *moving to hear better*. **Move2Hear** (arXiv 2105.07142): an RL agent moves to improve target-sound separation, rewarded for resolving the target from distractors. **ActiveRIR** (arXiv 2404.16216): RL navigation to choose acoustic *sampling positions*, rewarded by information gain in an acoustic-environment model. *"Real-World RL of Active Perception Behaviors"* (arXiv 2512.01188, NeurIPS 2025) generalizes the POMDP active-perception formulation. The robotics tradition (Nakadai et al., robot audition / CASA, doi 10.1002/aisy.202000050) has had "active audition = use motion to improve audition" for two decades. And **AAD-LLM** (arXiv 2502.16794) is a fresh, different flavor — *listener-attention-driven* scene understanding, where decoded attentional state conditions machine listening.

**What's missing (and it's a clean hole):** none of the above is active listening *as a learning objective on the signal itself*. There is no widely-cited audio model that, given a fixed recording, **chooses where/when/at what resolution to attend next so as to minimize its own predictive error** — the literal Friston active-inference loop applied to the time-frequency plane (foveated/glimpsed listening, learned STFT-window adaptation, uncertainty-driven temporal sampling). The embodied work moves a *body* in a *room*; the missing work would move *attention* in a *signal*. This is the sparsest area in the whole memo and the one most directly implied by the "predictive, thick-now" thesis. (It is also why Experiment 1 is framed as a probe rather than a full active-listener: the full thing is a multi-year program; the probe is weeks.)

## §8. Cross-modal grounding

**Sharp claim, because the brief asks specifically about *temporal* cognition, not task performance broadly: audio↔language grounding is overwhelmingly *semantic/atemporal* and does little for temporal cognition; audio↔motor and audio↔video-synchrony grounding are *inherently temporal* and are where cross-modal training demonstrably buys temporal structure.**

**Audio↔language is the wrong tool for time.** CLAP (arXiv 2206.04769) aligns audio with text captions in a joint space — excellent for *what is this sound* (tagging, retrieval), structurally indifferent to *when/in-what-order*. The tell-tale evidence is corrective work: **T-CLAP** (arXiv 2404.17806, "Temporal-Enhanced CLAP") exists precisely because base CLAP is temporally weak — captions like "a dog barks then a car passes" collapse to bags of concepts. So the *negative* claim is supported by the community's own patch.

**Audio↔motor is where temporal cognition is grounded.** Sensorimotor synchronization (tapping/dancing to a beat) is the human prior; the engineering instantiation is **MotionBeat** (arXiv 2510.13244, Oct 2025): a *motion-aligned* music representation via embodied contrastive learning with bar-equivariant, contact-aware encoding. The temporally-specific evidence is the transfer result: an encoder trained to align music with *movement* then **transfers to beat tracking and audio-visual retrieval** — i.e., motor grounding improved a *timing* task, not just its own objective. That is the cleanest existing example of cross-modal training improving temporal cognition specifically.

**Audio↔video-synchrony is temporal by construction.** Audio-visual sync as a self-supervised signal *is* a temporal-alignment objective. CrissCross (arXiv 2111.05329, AAAI 2022) shows that *relaxing* strict synchrony (learning both synchronous and asynchronous cross-modal relations) yields stronger representations — a nuance worth carrying: the temporal signal helps, but enforcing it too rigidly hurts.

**Honest caveat.** The MotionBeat transfer is one paper and recent; the strong claim ("motor grounding *causally* improves temporal cognition") rests on a thin base and deserves replication. But the *direction* is well-motivated and the audio↔language-is-atemporal point is robust.

## §9. Open problems (genuinely unsolved)

1. **Phase-as-cognition.** No representation treats monaural phase as a *downstream temporal-event feature* rather than a reconstruction target. The frame-length result (§1) says fine timing lives in phase that standard windows discard; whether a model that *reads* phase outperforms a magnitude model on timing tasks is untested as a general proposition (anti-spoofing is the only clear positive, and it's niche).

2. **Learned multi-timescale simultaneity.** No audio architecture *learns* a continuum of time constants resolving ms-microtiming and minutes-form in one pass. Current multi-scale is imposed by pooling schedules (SaShiMi) or fixed dual windows (SongFormer); Mamba's shared-Δ is a single timescale. Per-state-Δ SSMs are the right idea with no audio demonstration.

3. **Predictive ≠ proven for temporal structure.** Predictive objectives (CPC/APC/JEPA) demonstrably yield good *classification* representations; there is **no** demonstration that a predictive objective beats masked reconstruction on a task whose difficulty is specifically *long-range temporal*. The framing is widely assumed, never isolated.

4. **The measurement gap.** Temporal cognition is largely unmeasured: mainstream benchmarks cap at 10 s–2 min (§5), and there is no standard benchmark for microtiming/groove prediction against human ratings, nor for minutes-scale structural prediction. You cannot demonstrate (or refute) the memo's thesis on the current benchmark set.

5. **Active listening on the signal.** No model closes a Friston-style active-inference loop *within* a fixed signal (uncertainty-driven choice of where/when/at-what-resolution to attend). The embodied "move to hear" line is healthy; the internal-sampling line is empty (§7).

6. **The brain-alignment plateau (audio-specific).** Best DNNs sit well below the noise ceiling on representational similarity and "not much above" a hand-built spectrotemporal baseline (§4). *What computation is missing* is unidentified — and the noise-vs-clean training result hints the missing piece is in the *objective/environment*, not the architecture.

## §10. Three high-leverage experiments (1–3 GPUs, weeks)

For each: **hypothesis · design · falsifier · confirmer.** Experiment 3 is the non-obvious one.

### Experiment 1 — The phase-blindness probe (tests the category-error claim directly)
- **Hypothesis.** Spectrogram-derived models (AudioMAE / A-JEPA) are structurally blind to magnitude-preserving phase manipulations that are *perceptually and temporally meaningful* (transient smearing, onset asynchrony), whereas waveform models (wav2vec2 / SaShiMi-style) retain sensitivity. If true, "spectrogram = category error for time" is concretely demonstrated, not asserted.
- **Design.** Build a controlled timing-discrimination set: pairs differing only in *phase* with **magnitude spectrogram held (near-)constant** — e.g., onset-asynchrony between two concurrent notes (5/10/20/40 ms), attack-time/transient sharpness, click-train regularity. Freeze each pretrained model; train only a linear probe. Compare probe accuracy across (a) waveform vs spectrogram frontends and (b) STFT window length (sweeping the §1 frame-length axis). Cost: linear probes on frozen models — trivially 1 GPU, days.
- **Falsifier.** If spectrogram models match waveform models on magnitude-matched timing discrimination, *or* if phase-randomization (magnitude-preserved) leaves all models' timing discrimination unchanged, the strong category-error claim is false.
- **Confirmer.** A monotone interaction: spectrogram-model deficit grows as the discriminating information moves into phase / as windows lengthen, while waveform models stay flat.
- *Why it's worth running:* it converts the memo's central rhetorical claim into a measurable quantity, and either result is publishable (a null is a genuine correction).

### Experiment 2 — Multi-timescale by learning vs by construction
- **Hypothesis.** A single SSM layer with a *learned bank of time constants* (per-state Δ initialized across decades, 1 ms–10 s) outperforms both vanilla shared-Δ Mamba and a hand-tiered downsampling hierarchy (SaShiMi-style) **on a task that requires fine and coarse resolution simultaneously** — and crucially does so *without* the hand-set pooling schedule.
- **Design.** Task = joint microtiming + structure prediction on aligned music: predict next-onset *microtiming deviation* (fine, from Groove MIDI / E-GMD) **and** next *section boundary* (coarse, from a structure-annotated set, e.g., SongFormBench-style or Harmonix-style annotations) from the same representation. Three matched models at equal parameter count: (A) shared-Δ Mamba, (B) SaShiMi tiers, (C) multi-Δ Mamba (per-state Δ, log-spaced init). 1–3 GPUs, ~1–2 weeks.
- **Falsifier.** If shared-Δ Mamba matches multi-Δ on *both* scales, the shared-timescale limitation is not binding for audio (multi-scale is "assumed" but unnecessary). If SaShiMi tiers win, hand-construction beats learning and the §2 critique is wrong.
- **Confirmer.** Multi-Δ matches the hierarchy on coarse structure while beating it on microtiming (or vice-versa) — i.e., it captures *both* scales where single-scale models trade one off against the other; inspect the learned Δ distribution for multi-modality across decades.

### Experiment 3 (non-obvious) — Groove as a falsifiable test of the predictive-coding-of-time thesis
- **Hypothesis.** Musical groove is a *predictive-coding* phenomenon over microtiming: human-rated "desire to move / pleasure" tracks a model's **predictive surprise** about microtiming in an **inverted-U** (Witek 2014 / Berlyne). A *time-native predictive* model's microtiming prediction-error will correlate with human groove ratings in that inverted-U shape; a magnitude-spectrogram model and a non-predictive (reconstruction-only) model will **not** (the spectrogram can't see the deviations; the non-predictive model has no surprise signal). This simultaneously tests "predictive," "time-native," and "music is the benchmark."
- **Design.** Train a small autoregressive predictive model on Groove MIDI / E-GMD to predict next-onset microtiming deviation (continuous ms offset from grid), yielding per-event predictive *uncertainty* and *surprise*. Generate (or re-use) drum-break stimuli spanning low→high syncopation/microtiming complexity; obtain human groove ratings (replicate Witek-style ratings, or use existing groove-rating datasets, e.g., Senn et al. / the Witek stimulus set). Correlate model surprise with ratings. Controls: (a) magnitude-mel model, (b) waveform model with reconstruction-only objective, (c) IDyOM-style symbolic baseline as a *predictive* upper-anchor. 1 GPU; the model is tiny — the work is in stimulus/rating curation.
- **Falsifier.** No relationship between model surprise and groove ratings; *or* the relationship is equally strong for the non-predictive and magnitude baselines (then "groove = predictive coding" and the time-nativeness premium are both unsupported); *or* the relationship is linear, not inverted-U (then Berlyne/optimal-complexity is the wrong model).
- **Confirmer.** Inverted-U between model surprise and human ratings that is *present for the time-native predictive model and absent/weaker for spectrogram and non-predictive controls* — direct evidence that (i) groove is predictive-coding, (ii) the relevant signal is temporal-fine (phase/microtiming, invisible to mel), and (iii) a predictive objective is what surfaces it. This is the experiment that, if it works, makes the strongest single case for the whole memo — and if it fails cleanly, it's the most informative refutation.
